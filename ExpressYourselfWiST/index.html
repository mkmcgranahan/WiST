1st Annual Mary Kate McGranahan Cross Country Invitational

Welcome to the first every Mary Kate McGranahan Cross Country Invitational. This will be a 6k race, the standard collegiate women's cross country distance. Here is your bib, make sure to pin it to your singlet, tie your shoes, and get ready to go!
Instructions:
  • Press the hare to move forward in the race and run to the next marker.
  • Press the tortoise to move backward in case you missed anything, after all sometimes slow and steady wins the race
Looks like you're ready. Just a warning, this is not a normal cross country race. You'll have to read all the distance markers along the way to learn more about me and why you're running this race today.

On your mark... Go!

The Start

I was born on September 18, 2000 in Oakton, Virginia. As you can tell from my shades, I was very cool from the start.
When I was 5, my family moved to New York City. Sports helped me make friends in the concrete jungle. I played basketball and soccer.

1km

While my basketball career consisted of many 0 point seasons, I found success in being able to run up and down the soccer field without getting tired. At the same time, my dad started competing in Ironman triathlons. I decided I wanted to do what he did and entered kids triathlons. The running part of the triathlon was my favorite. In middle school, I joined my first track team. The furthest race we ran was the 800m, but I knew I was built for more distance so I ran 4 mile road races in Central Park with my dad and brother.
As much as I enjoyed the thrill of racing, I still loved the team camaraderie of soccer. I captained my 7th and 8th grade travel teams. When I got to high school, I knew cross country was the sport for me. I would be able to compete at a higher level in running, while still having the team aspect I loved so much. My dream was to compete in college

Warning, Hills Ahead!

This has been a pretty smooth race so far, but look at that big hill up ahead.
Let's take a quick detour to review some lessons I learned from cross country camp that helped me up all the hills in my running career. I went to Brevard Distance Runners camp as a camper for four years, and later went on to be a counselor. Every year at camp we did a challenging run that went four miles straight up a mountainside. This climb was difficult, but it was also the trail I mae the most friends on. I would find myself running next to state champions and All-Americans, both boys and girls. We were strangers from different schools and states at the time, but would encourage each other up the hills to the amazing views at the top. I realized that on the hills it wasn’t about who was the fastest, but who was willing to persevere and keep making small steps towards the top. No one asked you for your finish times at the end of those runs, it was all about the journey.

2k

This race is getting kind of serious and so was I. My junior year of high school I started training with the boys cross country team in order to have more equally matched training partners. It was supposed to be a great opportunity for me, but instead I overtrained every day because I was afraid of failing in front of the boys team. I didn’t take care of myself properly. Eventually, I realized this was wearing me down and I was not getting any faster. I adjusted my mentality, and began to see the boys as my equals, not people I needed to prove myself against. I split my time more evenly between the boys and girls team. Both teams helped me learn to have fun again and the whole process taught me that sometimes easy days help you move forward.
Only after I learned these lessons did I start to get faster and see results. I had a successful senior year and vowed to spend the rest of my career encouraging other female athletes to take care of their mental and physical health, while also achieving their goals.

Phew, you made it up the hill!

Need some mid-run fuel to celebrate? Lloyd's carrot cake is located right across the street from Van Cortlandt Park in the Bronx, where I ran every cross country meet in high school. It was a team tradition in high school to get a cake every year with a cross country pun on it. Lloyd’s always make me think of the wonderful girls who helped me have fun with the sport again. Have a piece to keep you going!
Another staple of Van Cortlandt Park is its tortoise and the hare statue, do those symbols look familiar?

3k

You're halfway there, which means its time to explore some new trails! In high school I began to explore Computer Science. I loved having a class where I got hands-on experience designing and build things, like this project that would later become a tetris board. I also realized Computer Science gave me the opportunity to work in any field I was interested in, including building technology for the sports that helped me grow into the person I am. I found the same lessons I had learned while running with the boys cross country team would help me persevere through negative gender stereotypes in Computer Science.
My senior year of high school, I committed to run Division III at Amherst College. The opportunity to run in college was a dream come true. I loved that Amherst was a place where I could develop as an athlete, while also being able to achieve my academic goals and double major in Computer Science and Economics.

4km

When I started at Amherst, I was a freshman working hard just to be one of the seven girls on the team chosen to compete at Regionals for my team. However, I finally had consistent training and was motivated by what I saw our men's team achieving. The men qualified for the national championship every year, and I wanted the women to be able to have that experience too. My career was abruptly interrupted halfway through my freshmen year by Covid. I spent over a year doing all of my runs alone, with no races in sight. That is, until one of the older girls on the team encouraged me to train for and run my first marathon with her. She helped me find the motivation I needed to stay positive during a hard time. She reconnected me with my team and my love for the sport.
I returned to Amherst my junior year, in the best shape of my life. I was the top runner on the team, third in the region, and led the women's team to its first national championship appearance since 2014.

5km

As I was hitting my stride in running, I was also gaining confidence in Computer Science. The summer between my junior and senior year I worked my first Software Engineering Internship at Garmin. I was excited to be working for a company whose running watches I had been using since I was a kid. I was assigned to work in IT on the Edge Technology team, a team that dealt with topics I knew nothing about. I quickly learned how my team kept Garmin’s entire network of websites and apps connected. By the end of the summer, I had designed and built a web application, called the Edge Debugging Tool, that helped Garmin developers analyze their applications’ DNS routing. I used Node.js to develop a back-end API that made calls to both proxied public and non-proxied internal DNS resource records and built out an educational user interface.
I was lucky enough to meet some great training partners in the intern class at Garmin. My new friends made waking up at 5am to run in the Kansas heat before work a lot easier and helped convince me to set big goals for the upcoming cross country season.

6km- The finish

My senior year I was the cross country conference champion and a cross country All-American. I qualified for my first national championship on the track in the 3k steeplechase, an event that is all about perseverance and being the grittiest one in the race. My favorite thing my senior year was realizing I had successfully created a team where the girls under me who did not have to grow up idolizing a mens team. They were instead motivated by watching their own teammates. In addition, it warmed my heart when freshmen boys started coming to me for training advice and support, seeing me as equal to their male captains.
You might think we’re at the end of the race now, I got my All-American medal and my dream internship. I graduated with my Bachelor’s in Computer Science and Economics. However, there’s a reason this is the 1st Mary Kate McGranahan Cross Country Invitational and not the last. You finished this race… but we’re not done yet!

Beyond the finish

My senior year encouraged me to raise my standards for myself. It showed me how much more there was for me to accomplish, in running and Computer Science. I committed to run Division I at Virginia Tech while getting my Master’s in Computer Science. I returned to Garmin for a second internship, but this time to work on the Global Partnerships and Engineering Business Development team, using my interest in business in and my technology experience to learn how a company I admire makes product decisions. I had another great summer of training with my fellow interns.
This fall, I finished my last collegiate cross country season in one of the most competitive conferences in the country. Now I have three more track seasons to see what I can do. I've started a new race and my next finish line involves a career in sports technology. A WiST fellowship would give me the opportunity to work with a new sports technology company to gain more experience and a better picture of the field as a whole. In addition, it would connect me to more female role models in sports technology. I am extremely excited for my next race, and hope that you are too.
